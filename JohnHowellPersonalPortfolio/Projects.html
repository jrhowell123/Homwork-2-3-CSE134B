    Global Warming's Potential Correlation with Shark Attacks

    This project examines the relatedness of unprovoked shark attacks with rising sea level and ocean temperature. Our goal was to find evidence proving that our hypothesis of there being a relationship between the two variables ocean temperature and sea level with shark attacks is true. Through the cleaning and analysis of two data sets, one looking at the progression of the ocean, and the other at shark attacks, we discovered that there is indeed a positive correlation of sea level and ocean temperature with shark attacks.

    Resaerch Question

    Is there a statistically significant correlation between rising ocean temperature and sea level with the frequency of unprovoked shark attacks in North America?

    Characterization of the Methane Seep Community at Lasuen Knoll

    MethaneSeeps are areas where reduced chemicals (methane and hydrogen sulfide) are seeping out of the earth and hotspots of biological activity. This study undertakes the investigation of Lasuen Knoll, a seep in the Southern California Borderland, the megafaunal community structure including density, taxonomic composition, distribution, diversity and lifestyles of the fauna will be fleshed out by comparing the active seep, transition, and background environments. This project started with a cruise on the RV Falkor focused on characterizing benthic biodiversity on different substrates in the Southern California Borderland. Then, post cruise analysis was done using stills and transects that were recorded on the dive at Lasuen Knoll. In transect analysis, an organism that is labeled as solitary coral was most abundant at roughly 8164. Munidopsis girguisi

    Munidopsis girguisi

    These crabs have chemosynthetic bacteria on their setae that are likely used as a food source for the arthropod. Since the crabs rely heavily on the source of methane, their abundance could give a sense of how much methane is being released at a given region within the seep

    was the second most abundant by count in the transects at 61. Furthermore, Iophon nr. methanophila sp. sponge, Munidopsis girguisi, folliculinid ciliate mat, and Solitary Coral were labeled as the overarching megafauna of the core seep environment. After looking at the results, two major questions came up. First, are the Munidopsis girguisi with bacteria covered claws that live at the core seep environment a different species than the Munidopsis girguisi found outside of the core seep area that do not show hairy bacteria covered claws? Second, are the solitary coral color morph types different species or a variation of the same species? In conclusion, Lasuen Knoll sets itself apart from the other seeps of the Southern California Borderland due to its shallow location and the many unanswered questions that still surround it.
